Burn Pits

a brief exploration by Jack MapelLentz

It's quite simple in theory: what goes in must go back out; what is thrown away must go somwhere. The wartime solution is surprisingly rudimentary — throw everything in one spot and light it on fire. Burn pits, as they came to be called, are designated areas at military bases at which all sorts of discarded things are dumped, doused in an accelerant, and burned at blazingly hot temperatures. They range from holes in the ground (like the cover photo) to metal drums, as in this 1991 photo from the Gulf War [Leonard J. DeFrancisci/Wikipedia].

Some US bases are surprisingly well-furnished, I have found. Joint Base Balad, for one, had a movie theatre complete with a built-in Subway.

KBR, a government contractor — we will return to them shortly — operated hospitals and clinics, which housed massive stores of medicines.

But all of those things — the discarded candy wrappers, the expired medications, old uniforms, everything — had to be burned.

Jennifer Percy, writing in The New Republic, captured the extent of the burning more powerfully than I ever could in her article “The Things they Burned”, so I am just going to relay its intro paragraph:

“Specialist Nicolas Plantiko burned dogs. Sergeant Thomas J. Brennan burned lithium ion batteries, flame-resistant FROG suits, and MK-19 rounds. He burned plastic chemical drums, nylon, tires, wires, and tarps. He burned [feces] and [urine]. Sergeant Bill Moody’s unit burned a Porta-John, dried-up MREs, and 500 loaves of moldy bread. Staff Sergeant Louis Levesque burned bunk beds. Private Johnnie Stevenson burned plastic bottles because he loved the way they hissed. Airborne infantryman Dennis St. Pierre burned radio batteries and chemlights. Sergeant Carlos Castro joked about burning another soldier for talking too much. Captain Matthew Frye burned a packet of Tabasco sauce that exploded and nearly took out the JTAC’s eye. Staff Sergeant Tim Wymore burned 25 loads of DEET-soaked tents and walked around with the taste of smoke in his mouth. Sergeant Zachary Bell burned batteries because the Taliban used the carbon rods for IED triggers. Specialist Dante Sowell burned burlap bags so he wouldn’t have to fill them up with sand. Captain Adrian Bonenberger watched a Christmas tree go into a burn pit. Private George Snyder burned Private Stuart Decker’s one confirmed kill. Sergeant Casey Rohrich burned a human toe. They burned magazines, movies, junk food, college brochures, and pamphlets for the GI Bill. They burned amputated body parts and Humvee parts. They burned human waste and plastic meal trays. They burned the blood and clothes of the wounded.”

Burn pits were and are found all over the world, but Iraq, Afghanistan, and, to a lesser extent, Kuwait have by far been home to the most. One website, VetsHQ, compiled a list of over 300 open burn pits, of which 152 are/were in Iraq, 99 in Afghanistan, and 14 in tiny Kuwait. As one might expect, there arose many problems from the mass-burning of refuse — for one, the jet fuel used to ignite them released benzene, a very well-known carcinogen. (Note the numerous warning labels on the right of this bottle of benzene.)

The entirety of Leon Russell Keith's testimony to the Senate is worth reading. He was a paramedic with KBR and worked in Iraq from 2006-2007, again deploying from 2008-2009. Here is an excerpt:

"While I was stationed at Balad I experienced the effects of the massive burn pit that burned 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. The ten-acre pit was located in the northwest corner of the base. An acrid, dark black smoke from the pit would accumulate and hang low over the base for weeks at a time. Every spot on the base was touched by smoke from the pit; everyone who served at the base was exposed to the smoke. It was almost impossible to escape, even in our living units. Ash from the smoke would seep into the air conditioning systems and our living areas would be covered in a coating of dark soot. Our rooms had what looked like dark-colored flour spread over everything, including our beds, our clothing and the floor. We called this 'Iraqi talcum powder.'"

"There was nothing that KBR would not put in the burn pits."

Keith now suffers from neurological damage and, his doctor says, exhibits signs of Parkinson's disease. "The pain I have from the muscular contractions can be excruciating," he said.

Below, a burn pit at Balad airbase in Balad, Iraq, March 2008 [US Air Force/Reuters]

If it hasn't become obvious by now, KBR is a particularly problematic player in the burn pit saga. It’s a private company that primarily operates as a contractor — it makes its money off of winning government contracts, sometimes employing questionable means to do so. It was spun off from the acquisition of Dresser Industries by energy giant Halliburton in 1998. Its contract-winning roots stretch back to World War II and through the Vietnam War to bring us to the Iraq War, during which many of these high-profile burn pits operated. I was startled to read that one KBR employee, a lieutenant colonel in the Army Reserve by the name of Rick Lamberth, testified that his supervisors told him to “shut up and keep it to myself” after voicing concerns about the company’s burn pit practices. It turns out this is by no means out of the ordinary for KBR.

As mentioned, KBR has a history of sketchy endeavors. Lyndon B. Johnson had an arrangement with KBR’s predecessor by which he pushed for their being awarded contracts in return for their donating money to his campaign. More recently, during the Iraq War, one Defense Department official was suddenly ousted after he refused to approve over $1 billion in charges by KBR for which the Army had deemed its records and justification insufficient. Abroad, KBR plead guilty in 2009 to bribing Nigerian government officials to approve construction of a natural gas facility. In 2008, an American soldier — Sergeant Ryan Maseth — died after being electrocuted in a shower stall. KBR installed faulty wiring across other US bases, and was given a Level III Corrective Action Request — only one item away from termination of a contract. It was awarded a $35 million contract for more electrical work the next year.

A subcontractor of KBR’s in Jordan was accused of human trafficking in one 2008 court case. I’m not even going to go into the details. It’s too horrifying.

These burn pits operated for some eighteen years or more — in 2019, nine burn pits remained throughout Syria, Afghanistan, and Egypt. Though their prevalence has declined, the United States is now faced with scores of soldiers chronically ill with a smorgasbord of diseases, likely from exposure to burn pits.

The government’s response, though, has been, to put it very lightly, extremely lackluster. The similarities to its reaction to the health effects of Agent Orange are striking, as noted by Percy: it first refused to acknowledge that there might be anything wrong with the practice, insisting that it was the safest, cheapest, and most effective method of disposing waste; then, it relented to government pressure (partially from Minnesota’s own Amy Klobuchar; Joe Biden, also, has publicly speculated that his son Beau’s death from cancer was caused in part by burn pit exposure) and begun conducting studies, but those studies take years upon years and are so often ill-architected, if not outright manipulated. “We can’t let burn pits become the new Agent Orange,” wrote senators Klobuchar and Thom Tillis of Arizona in a Fox News op-ed.

In the meantime, the VA denied request after request for medical care related to burn pits. Dozens of affected people spit up mucus on the daily. Still more can no longer complete the two-mile run that serves as the basic benchmark of physical fitness for deployment. At the time of Percy’s article’s publishing in 2016, though, the VA’s website read that “At this time, research does not show evidence of long-term health problems from exposure to burn pits.” One VA hospital told a veteran’s wife that he was only experiencing PTSD, which, they said, explained his tumors and basal cell carcinoma.

At Vanderbilt University, Robert Miller researched lung biopsies of veterans exposed to burn pits. He found that many had bronchiolitis — an often-fatal swelling of the bronchioles and subsequent closure of the small airways. Meanwhile, the VA funded studies that found inconclusive result after inconclusive result, despite its conflict of interest — if a study found decisive results, it would be on the hook for millions of dollars of insurance coverage. The military stopped sending soldiers to Miller after his findings showed bronchiolitis.

Things may finally be trending in the positive direction, though. In 2017, the Supreme Court ruled that veterans could sue KBR over its operation of the sites. A banner on the VA’s page on burn pits reads that “On April 25, 2022, VA announced that nine rare respiratory cancers are now presumed service-connected disabilities due to military environmental exposures to fine particulate matter.“ And in 2021, it notably added asthma, rhinitis, and sinusitis to its list of conditions presumptively tied to particulate matter exposure.

But many have already died from cancers and respiratory illnesses, and others likely will even in the coming months alone. For some, it’s too little, too late.
